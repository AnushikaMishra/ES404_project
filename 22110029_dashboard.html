What is the Cerebrovascular Network?  The cerebrovascular network is the intricate web of blood vessels — arteries, arterioles, capillaries, and venules — that supplies oxygen and nutrients to the brain. It is modelled as a spatial graph where nodes are vessel bifurcation points and edges are vessel segments with a measured radius attribute (µm). Studying this network using graph theory reveals how the brain sustains robust blood flow, and how disruptions like stroke or vascular aging propagate through this life-critical system.

Critical finding:The network exhibits high Targeted Vulnerability. Despite a low maximum degree (k_{max}=5), the system collapses significantly faster under targeted hub removal than random failure. This is due to the Near-Tree Topology: because the network lacks redundant loops, these degree 4–5 nodes act as "bottlenecks." Removing them doesn't just remove a node; it orphans entire downstream vascular branches, proving that even in low-degree biological networks, specific structural "hubs" are vital for system-level integrity.
